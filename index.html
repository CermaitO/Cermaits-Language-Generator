What is Kerbekulo?

Kerbekulo is a fictional language, made up purely for the purposes of this generator, which I made to practice JavaScript while indulging in my love of languages. Kerbekulo has a fixed set of affixes and a fixed morphophonology, however I have not created any words because that would take the fun away from this generator! Phonology

Kerbekulo has five vowel qualities: /i, u, e ,o , a/, and two vowel quantities: long and short. Long vowels only appear as a result of sound changes which will be discussed below.
There is also the vowel schwa ə but this only occurs as a result of the syllabification of l and r and thus is not a phonemic vowel.
The consonants are:

  • Nasals: /m, n/
  • Plosives: /p, b, t, d, k, g/
  • Fricatives: /f, s, h/
  • Sonorants: /r, l/
  • Approximants: /j, w/

Kerbekulo has the following possible root structures (C = consonant, V = vowel):

  • CV: -gu-
  • CVC: -mud-
  • CVCV: -weri-
  • CVCVC: -regon-
  • CVCC: -murk-
  • CVCCV: -dormu-

Morphophonology

When different morphemes are concatenated together, such as attaching a prefix or suffix, or compounding two roots together, several sound changes occur. Such that the root -gu- "eye" with the singular suffix -ko and accusative prefix he- does not create *heguko but hēhko. The rules are as follows.

Syncope

In a word that is at least three syllables long, the vowel in the second syllable is lost and the vowel in the first syllable becomes long: he-lu-ko > hēlko, weri-ko > wērko.
However, if the vowel of the second syllable is surrounded by two of the same consonant, then it is the vowel of the third syllable that is lost and the vowel of the second syllable becomes long: rako-ko > rakōk.

In a word that is two syllables long or more, but the first syllable is heavy (has a consonant following the vowel), then the first consonant of the second syllable is lost and the vowel of the first syllable becomes long. The first consonant of the second syllable is also lost: gim-ko > gīmo. For such nouns, the singular suffix -ko and plural suffix -te become -o and -te: -gim- > gīmo, gīme
If the first syllable was super-heavy (has two consonants following the vowel) then the second consonant of the first syllable is lost: pand-ko > pānko.

Lenition

Kerbekulo does not allow a plosive to cluster before a plosive of the same place of articulation. when such clusters occur, the first plosive lenites to a voiceless fricative. If the second plosive was voiced, it devoices after the fricative

  • pb, bp > fp: he-lob-perg-ko > hēlbperko > hēlfperko; he-wirpbego-ko > hēurpbegoko > hēurfpegoko
  • dt, td > st: mud-te > muste; he-ritdolm-ko > hērtdolmgo > hērstolmgo
  • kg, gk > hk: bego-ko > bēgko > bēhko

Metathesis

The cluster bm becomes mb: durib-melon-ko > dūrmbelonko

Voicing of mC clusters

Voiceless plosives become voiced when after m.

  • mp > mb: kerem-parki-ko > kērmbarkiko
  • mt > md: barum-te > bārmde
  • mk > mg: pergo-losem-ko > pērolosemgo

Syllabification

If w or j are made to occur before a consonant, as a result of syncope, then they syllabify into u and i respectively: dawo-ko > dāwko > dāuko; suje-te > sūjte > sūite.

The sonorants r and l syllabify into a schwa ə but only when they are both before and after a consonant. The schwa is unaffected by syncope, as this syllabification occurs after syncope takes place: he-mul-ko > hēmlko > hēməko; tebor-te > tēbrte > tēbəte.

Loss of /h/ After Consonants

The consonant /h/, which occurs in Kerbekulo exclusively as a result of kg, gk > hk, is lost when it occurs after another consonant: sebeg-ko > sēbgko > sēbhko > sēbko.

Shortening of Geminates

Two of the same consonants may cluster together as a result of compounding. Kerbekulo avoids these clusters by shortening the geminate consonants into one single short consonant: balis-sol-ko > bālssolko > bālsolko

Morphology

Noun case is marked with two prefixes: for the accusative and for the genitive. The nominative case is unmarked.

Demonstrative Pronouns

Demonstrative pronouns have a number distinction ("this" vs "these") and a proximal distinction ("this" vs "that"). The proximal distinction is older. The pronouns and "that" both descend from adverbial forms of "here" and "there" respectively. The number distinction came simply by applying the normal plural suffixes to create "these" and "those".

  • "here" + "adverbial suffix" > "this" + "plural suffix" > "these"
  • "there" + "adverbial suffix" > "that" + "plural suffix" > "those"

Demonstratives also take on case prefixes, to agree with the noun that they govern. They are unlike nouns, in that they take no overt singular suffix when in the nominative case.

Plural Verbs

It may be noticed above, that no prefixes serving to mark the plural persons exist. The plural pronouns in Kerbekulo are simply the singular forms but with the standard plural suffix, which hints at their fairly young age. The usage of pronouns as verbal prefixes likely predates the formation of Kerbekulo's plural pronouns altogether, hence their absence. Instead, of having explicit plural forms for each person, Kerbekulo has plural verbs. The same prefixes for the singular persons applied to a plural verb serve to mark plural persons. A plural verb without any person-marking prefixes is assumed to mark the third person plural. A verb is made plural with the suffix -BLANK. The plural verb suffix applies before the non-past suffix.
